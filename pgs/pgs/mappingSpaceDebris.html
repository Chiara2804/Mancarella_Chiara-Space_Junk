You may be wondering, “What is Orbital Debris?”
Although we don't see space junk in the sky, beyond the clouds and farther than the eye can see, it enters Low Earth Orbit (LEO). LEO is a junk store space orbital. There are millions of pieces of space junk flying into LEO.
Most orbital debris includes man-made objects, such as pieces of spaceships, tiny bits of paint, rocket parts or satellites that no longer work. Most of the "space junk" moves very fast and can reach a speed of 28968.2 km/h.
Due to the speed and volume of debris in LEO, services, explorations and current and future space operations pose a security risk to people and property in space and on Earth. There are many reasons why LEO developed into an orbital cemetery. LEO is now considered the largest garbage dump in the world and it is expensive to remove space debris from LEO because the space garbage problem is huge --- there are nearly 6,000 tons of materials in low earth orbit.
The program NASA Orbital Debris officially began in 1979 in the Space Sciences Branch of the Johnson Space Center (JSC) in Houston, Texas. The program looks for ways to create less orbital debris and designs equipment to track and remove debris already in space. Space junk is not the responsibility of a single country, but the responsibility of every country that travels to space.
The problem of managing space debris is both an international and an opportunity to preserve the space environment for future space exploration missions. We have created a global problem that can only be solved with the help of other countries.
